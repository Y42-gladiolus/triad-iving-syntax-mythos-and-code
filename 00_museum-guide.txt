TITLE: Museum Guide: Why Look Into the Language Collection
STYLE: practical | for English speakers | no mysticism
PURPOSE: Help engineers extract value from "imperfect" experiments

================================================================================
INTRODUCTION: Side Solutions Are Often More Important Than Results
================================================================================

> "Sometimes you make one thing, get another — and that 'another' is more valuable."

Experiment is not just "success/failure". It is also:
  • Unexpected findings along the way
  • Partially usable pieces (even with 40% system error)
  • Understanding boundaries: "doesn't work here, because..."

Historical example:
> First nuclear test calculations were done with slide rules.
> 5–9x error margin? Yes. But — it worked.
> Because "approximately right" is sometimes better than "precisely never".

This repository is such a "museum of approximate truths".
No perfect languages here. Just ideas that:
  • Worked in their context
  • Left marks on modern tools
  • Wait for their time to come

================================================================================
PHILOSOPHY: Why Look Into the "Museum"?
================================================================================

> "Ideas don't die. They wait for their time."

Languages like Red, Io, Forth, Self, Oz — these are not "failed languages".
They are:
  • Experiments that超前 their time
  • Ideas now in Rust/Go/Zig — but in "purer" form
  • Sources of inspiration for non-classical solutions

You are not "studying a museum". You are **searching for ideas** that:
  • Haven't become mainstream yet
  • Can give breakthrough in your tasks
  • Wait for the moment when their time comes

When it "clicks" — you take an idea from the museum and implement it in Zig/Chez/CLP.
That is innovation.

The museum is open for researchers.

================================================================================
READING RULE: How to Extract Value from "Imperfect"
================================================================================

1. DON'T search for "ready production language"
   → Search for "idea that can be adapted"

2. DON'T compare "who is faster/more powerful"
   → Compare "what problem this language solved elegantly"

3. DON'T dismiss language for "outdated syntax"
   → Ask: "what thought did it express that's hard to express now?"

4. DON'T wait for "complete documentation"
   → Read code, experiment, draw conclusions

5. DON'T fear "40% system error"
   → If idea works at 60% — that's already material for thought

================================================================================
PRACTICAL ADVICE: How to Start Research
================================================================================

Step 1: Pick a language from classification (CLASSIFICATION.md)
Step 2: Open its "body" (01_*.txt or *-code.org) — see syntax
Step 3: Read its "soul" (*-fable.org) — understand context
Step 4: Run "action" (03_*/...) — test in practice
Step 5: Ask yourself:
  • "What from this can I take to my project?"
  • "Which idea here is worth stealing?"
  • "Where does this idea already work — and how?"

Step 6: Write conclusion — even if it's "doesn't fit, because..."
  → Negative result is also a result.

LYRICAL INTERLUDE #1: Shoelaces + Sand
================================================================================

> "Sometimes the right answer comes in wrong form."

LIFE STORY:
Once, in my youth, complained at my little daughter:
> "Shoelaces on boots keep untying. Fed up."

Child (who just learned to tie laces) suggested:
> "Smear them in sand. They do that in kindergarten — then they don't untie."

For me — that was an insight.

WHY IT WORKS (physical basis):
Sand particles create friction between lace fibers.
Knot "holds" not on weaving complexity — but on microscopic grip.
"Dirty" lace is more reliable than "clean" one.

MORPHOGENETIC MEANING:
> Child doesn't know knot theory.
> But knows practice: "sand = holds".
> This knowledge came not from books — from experience, environment, "collective field".

That is "mystical insight" with physical basis:
  • Simple solution to complex problem
  • Came from one who "shouldn't know"
  • Works, though "wrong" by textbook

PARALLEL WITH PROGRAMMING:
| Shoelaces + sand              | Code + "wrong" tool               │
|-------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| Looks "dirty"                 | Looks "unfashionable" (.txt vs .org)│
| But holds reliably            | But works everywhere              │
| Child knows, physicist — no   | Practitioner knows, theorist — argues│
| Solution from "wrong" source  | Solution from "wrong" language    │

TAKEAWAY FOR RESEARCHER:
> Don't dismiss solution because it's "ugly".
> Don't dismiss source because it's "unauthoritative".
> Ask: "does it work?" — before "is it correct?"

Sometimes truth comes in form that hurts the eye.
But if lace holds — means sand is in place.

LYRICAL INTERLUDE #2: Sacred Circus and Price of Belief
================================================================================

> "Sacralization — deliberate and conscious self-deception."
>                                                    (D.Yu., primary source)

ADVICE FROM ASSISTANT (can contain structured logic):
> "Believe in libraries. It's good and easy."

SCIENTIFIC PRINCIPLE (that everyone forgets):
> "Don't believe. Prove."

QUESTION: But what to prove? And mainly — how?

MATHEMATICS OF DELUSIONS: Price of "worked"

You wrote one and a half libraries for yourself. Know the pain.

Simple truth:
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ To have 99 out of 100 times "work"    → need 5,000 falls in testing        │
│ To have 999 out of 1000 times "work"  → need 1,000,000 falls               │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

Not metaphor. This is debugging statistics.

OTHER LIBRARIES (the "ready-made" ones):
  • Not written by people? → People wrote them.
  • Not in team? → In team.
  • Error probability? → Grows geometrically with "collectivization".

Collective error formula:
    P(error) = 1 - (1 - p)^n
    where p = one developer's error, n = number of developers

More "experts" — higher chance someone already deceived themselves.
And you — inherit this delusion through `import`.

PHYSICS OF BELIEF: Why brain economizes on logic

Origins of belief (convictions) have different logically grounded physical basis.

IN THE BRAIN:
  • Mathematical logic = expensive (energy, time, neurons)
  • Belief = cheap (ready template, no thinking needed)
  • Common sense = sometimes available, but rarely

BELIEF ECONOMY:
> "Why check library if 10,000 stars on GitHub?"
> "Why read code if tests are green?"
> "Why understand if it works?"

Not laziness. This is evolutionary optimization.
Brain saves resources. Belief is cache for convictions.

PROBLEM:
> Cache becomes stale.
> But you — continue believing.

OPTIMIZATION "SOLVERS": Where to break programs?

You not familiar with optimization algorithms of "solvers"?
Then question:

> On which structure or stream do you plan to break your programs?

Options:
  • Production? → Painful, expensive, shameful.
  • Tests? → Good, but tests don't cover "delusions".
  • Debug in head? → Better, but brain also caches errors.
  • Language museum? → Cheap, safe, can break others' ideas.

Choice is yours.

LYRICAL FINALE: Melancholy and Blues

> "But these are 'songs' about different — just melancholy and blues attacked..."

Sometimes need to admit:
  • Not everything can be proved.
  • Not everything worth checking.
  • Not everything "working" — is correct.
  • Not everything "correct" — works.

And that — is normal.

Blues is also part of process.
It signals: "You tired of believing. Start understanding."

TAKEAWAY FOR RESEARCHER (from this circus)

1. Belief in libraries — ok. But remember: people wrote them.
2. "Works" ≠ "Correct". Sometimes — opposite.
3. 5,000 falls is not failure. This is price of 99% reliability.
4. Brain economizes on logic. Not bug. This is evolution feature.
5. Blues is not enemy. This is signal: "Time to move from belief to understanding."

And main:
> "Sacred circus" is not sentence.
> This is environment we all work in.
> Realize it — already half of exit.
